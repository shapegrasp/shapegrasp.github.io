Abstract

Grasping unfamiliar objects is a necessary skill for robots in dynamic in-home environments. Inspired by the human capability to grasp unfamiliar objects through intuition about their shape and structure, we present a novel zero-shot grasping method based on geometric shape decomposition. Unlike prior methods which require additional semantic information about the target object, e.g., its name or affordance, our approach decomposes objects into a graph consisting of geometric shapes and spatial relationships. Given an object's graph, we leverage the commonsense reasoning abilities of large language models to identify an area suitable for grasping based on the object's structure. Through extensive experiments in simulation and on a real-world platform, we demonstrate that our zero-shot grasping approach is capable of picking objects by reasoning over their convex geometric decomposition, resulting in a success rate of 92%.
